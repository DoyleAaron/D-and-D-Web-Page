# The Bar

The Mogged Pigeon groaned under the weight of bad decisions.

Smoke clung to the crooked beams, the floor was the colour of old ale and regret, and a one-eyed bard in the corner was doing unspeakable things to a lute.

At a round table at the rooms corner, Amdir Aeristel smiled like a man who’d never once considered the gods might disapprove of him.

The high elf lounged with easy, arrogant grace, long pale hair spilling over a coat that cost more than most of the room would see in a month. Cards fanned between long fingers. A neat stack of coins glittered in front of him.

Across from him, a big-shouldered dockhand wiped sweat from his brow and stared at his last few coppers.

“Last hand,” the man muttered. “Double or nothing.”

Amdir tilted his head. “You don’t have ‘double’, friend. You barely have ‘something’.”

“Scared?” the dockhand growled.

Amdir’s smile sharpened. “Of free money? Never. Deal.”

They played in silence broken only by the crack of dice at nearby tables and the bard missing yet another note. Amdir didn’t bother hiding his smirk as he laid out his final hand.

“Three queens,” he said. “How unfortunate for you.”

The dockhand slapped his own cards down. Two pair. Not enough. His jaw worked.

“You cheated.”

“Of course I did,” Amdir said mildly, sweeping the last of the coins into his pouch. “You thought you were going to _win_ at cards in a dockside tavern against a stranger in silk? That’s not cheating, that’s education.”

A few nearby listeners snorted. The dockhand went red.

“Elf bastard,” he spat, shoving his chair back so hard it nearly toppled.

Amdir didn’t flinch. “Thank you for your contribution to the Amdir Aeristel Survival Fund. It will be spent irresponsibly.”

The man stormed off, shoulder-checking a serving lad and nearly taking the door off its hinges on the way out.

Amdir leaned back, listening to the coins clink as he idly bounced the pouch in his hand. The sound was better than the bard’s music.

---

By the fire, the night felt almost cosy.

Morjor the Enlightened sat straight but relaxed on a bench, frame folded neatly, hands around a clay mug. Beside him, Varin loomed like a green boulder in bahamut robes, scarred face softened by the firelight.

Across from them, Old Brann waved his tankard dangerously near the flames.

“I swear on every saint you can name,” Brann slurred, “this thing was _huge_. Scales black as midnight, eyes like lanterns. River drake, they called it. I called it ‘bloody nuisance’ as it tried to eat my boat.”

Morjor’s eyes crinkled with polite interest. “And how did you survive such a mighty foe, friend Brann?”

Brann thumped his chest. “With these.” He flexed an arm that had not seen honest work in some years. “And a frying pan.”

Varin’s heavy brows lifted. “You beat a drake,” he said, voice low and warm, “with cookware.”

“Best pan I ever owned,” Brann insisted. “Lost it in the fight. Tragedy, that. Thing had a perfect sear.”

Morjor hid a smile behind his mug. “All tools can become weapons in the right hands.”

Varin’s gaze stayed on the old man, gentle despite the tusks and scars. “Or in the wrong ones,” he said. “The gods judge the heart, not the pan.”

Brann squinted at him. “You one o’ them holy types, then?”

Varin shrugged one massive shoulder. “I follow the Light,” he said simply. “Try to be better than what I was born as.”

Brann studied the half-orc’s face for a beat, then grinned.

“Then buy us another ale, Your Holiness. Share the Light around, eh?”

Morjor chuckled softly. “He has you there, brother.”

Varin sighed, but there was the echo of a smile on his mouth. “Fine. But only because my god frowns on letting old men dehydrate.”

He raised a hand to catch Tilda’s attention.

---

In the shadowed corner near the back wall, Tearius Dropius Pather watched everything and cared about very little of it.

Hood up, chair tilted back on two legs, he let a copper coin walk over his knuckles, vanish, reappear. The light from the hearth didn’t quite reach him; he seemed to prefer it that way.

He’d watched Amdir cheat the dockhand and mentally rated the performance. Too obvious, he decided. All flourish, not enough subtlety. That sort of thing got you stabbed in quieter cities than Sheyton.

He’d watched Varin and Morjor humour the old drunk and pegged them, again, as the sort who would throw themselves into trouble for anyone that asked nicely enough.

Useful people to stand behind. Inconvenient people to care about.

Tilda passed close to his table with a tray of drinks. Tearius flicked the copper; it arced neatly through the air and landed on the tray without touching a mug.

“For the song,” he said, nodding at the bard.

Tilda snorted. “Song’s not worth that much.”

“Exactly,” Tearius replied. “Think of the change as hazard pay.”

Tilda shook her head and moved on, but there was the faintest ghost of a smile at the corner of her mouth.

---

The door slammed open.

The one-eyed bard actually _stopped_ playing—that was how sharp the sound was. Every head turned.

A small girl in a ragged dress stood in the doorway, clutching a patched doll so tight the stitching strained. Her cheeks were streaked with tears and grime.

“Please!” she gasped. “Please, someone help!”

Tilda swore under her breath. “Gods damn it…”

Morjor was on his feet before the girl had finished the sentence, mug set down so gently it barely made a sound.

Varin rose a heartbeat later, bench creaking as he pushed off it. “Child,” he said, already moving towards her, “what’s wrong?”

Tearius did not move. He watched.

Amdir glanced up from counting his winnings, eyes narrowing. His first instinct was to weigh the room: the girl’s clothing—poor; the doll—well-loved but cheap; the crowd’s reaction—sympathetic but wary.

Poor folk didn’t pay for heroics.

The girl stumbled further in, eyes wide in the smoky light. “They hurt my mam,” she blurted. “They took her locket—my papa’s locket. She’s bleeding, she—she can’t get up—”

Her voice cracked. A few patrons muttered, exchanging looks that said _not my problem_.

Morjor knelt so he was on her level, his presence somehow smaller, less intimidating. “Breathe,” he said softly. “Slowly. You are safe here, little one. What is your name?”

“L-Lysa.”

“Lysa.” He nodded. “Where is your mother?”

“Outside,” she sniffed. “By the cart. They knocked her down and—and I ran here because people say the Guard comes here, and—”

She broke down again.

Varin laid a gentle hand on her shoulder, huge green fingers almost comically careful. “You did well to run,” he said. “The Light guides brave hearts.”

He looked over at Tilda. “We’ll see to her.”

Tilda’s mouth tightened. “I didn’t hire you,” she said. “And I don’t want blood on my floor.”

“We won’t bring it back in,” Varin replied. There was no heat in his tone, just certainty.

Morjor stood, giving Lysa a reassuring nod. “Come. Show us.”

They turned towards the door.

Amdir cleared his throat.

“Hold on,” he said. “Before everyone goes charging into the night like a sanctified stampede—”

Varin looked back, patience already thinning. “There’s a woman bleeding,” he said. “What else do you need?”

“Motivation,” Amdir said bluntly. “I make it a rule never to risk my delicate bones without a reason.”

Lysa stared at him, confused and horrified all at once.

“My mother’s going to _die_,” she whispered.

Amdir winced theatrically. “Yes, emotionally very compelling. But if I get stabbed out there, _my_ mother will be devastated, and she pays better than you do.”

Morjor’s jaw tightened. “Gold is not the only measure, Amdir.”

“No,” Amdir said. “But it is the only one that pays for meals, and my drinks are not cheap.”

Varin took a slow breath, like he was wrestling something heavier than his own muscles. When he spoke, his voice was steady.

“If we help,” he asked Lysa, “can your mother pay anything? Even a little?”

Lysa nodded frantically. “She… she has coin hidden. For the cart, for food. She’d give all of it if you just—”

“There,” Amdir said, straightening. “A contract. Retroactive, but I’m flexible.”

He rose and brushed imaginary dust from his sleeves. “Lead on, little Lysa. Let’s see what kind of trouble your misfortune has dragged us into.”

Morjor gave him a look that was half disappointment, half resignation.

“However you arrive,” the monk murmured, “you still arrive.”

“Exactly,” Amdir said. “Let’s go be noble and transactional.”

They moved towards the door, Varin keeping Lysa close, Morjor a quiet shadow behind them, Amdir strolling as though he had all the time in the world.

In the corner, Tearius watched them go.

He took a slow sip of his drink. He still didn’t stand.

People were robbed in Sheyton every night. Most of those stories began and ended without ever touching him. Life stayed cleaner that way.

The door swung shut behind Varin, Morjor, Amdir and the girl. The tavern noise crept back in—dice, low talk, the bard butchering another tune. The chair opposite Tearius scraped quietly across the floor.

He didn’t look up right away. The coin travelled over his fingers, vanished, reappeared.

“You’re really going to sit through this one?” a familiar voice asked, dry and precise. “Even for you, that’s committed laziness.”

Nehinhatu slid into the seat as if he’d paid for it. Oil and metal stained his hands and apron, but there was nothing dull in his eyes. They were sharp, focused, taking in everything.

Tearius finally glanced at him. “Most fires burn themselves out,” he said. “No profit in running into all of them.”

Nehinhatu studied him for half a heartbeat, then set a folded scrap of paper on the table between them, fingers resting on it for a moment.

“This isn’t one of those fires,” he said quietly. “Read that. Now.”

Tearius’s gaze flicked from the note to Nehinhatu’s face. There was tension there—not panic, but controlled worry. That was unusual.

“Since when do I get priority mail?” Tearius asked.

“Since people above my pay grade started whispering about a pendant in _your_ city,” Nehinhatu replied. “And since idiots with knives started shaking down anyone in a red scarf to find it.”

He lifted his hand from the paper and leaned back, as if the note were suddenly nothing at all.

Across the room, Tilda shouted at someone for spilling ale. The bard missed another chord. No one else seemed to notice the two men at the quiet table.

Tearius slid the note off the wood with two fingers, palming it in one smooth motion. He rose from his chair with that lazy, unhurried gait that meant he was actually paying attention.

Tilda called after him, “If you’re going to start trouble, do it outside my walls.”

“I’m going to avoid trouble,” Tearius said without looking back. “If I can.”

He slipped through the back door into the yard.

---

The air behind the Mogged Pigeon smelled of oil, wet stone, and old iron. A single, oddly elegant bicycle leaned against the wall, gleaming where the lantern light hit polished metal. It looked like it belonged in another world.

Nehinhatu was crouched beside it, checking a chain that absolutely did not need checking.

“You picked an interesting time to drop by,” Tearius said, letting the door click shut behind him.

“This is my workbench,” Nehinhatu replied without looking up. “You’re the one who treats it like a confessional.”

Tearius unfolded the note. The handwriting was quick and tight, clearly written in a hurry.

_They’re moving tonight. Old warehouse by the canal. Not a simple grab—orders from above. They’re hunting the **pendant**, putting pressure on Red Scarves to find it._

He read it twice, then lowered the paper.

“So the thieves outside,” he said slowly. “They weren’t after some sentimental trinket. They were checking a Red Scarf in case she had this pendant.”

Nehinhatu stood, wiping his hands on a rag that had long since given up. “That’s what I’m hearing,” he said. “Someone up the chain wants that pendant badly enough to lean on street crews. Red Scarf households are getting ‘visited’ until someone gets lucky.”

“They found nothing useful, so they took whatever they could grab. The locket’s just noise.”

“Exactly,” Nehinhatu said. “The girl out there thinks this is about her father’s locket. Those do-gooders, they’re walking blind into guild pressure and Crown attention.”

Tearius leaned back against the wall, folding the note between his fingers.

“You’re a busy man,” he said. “Private mechanic for the only bike in the world, part-time informant, full-time cynic. Why warn me?”

Nehinhatu gave him a look that mixed annoyance with something softer. “Because you have a talent for surviving bad situations,” he said. “And a talent for finding them. If people are kicking in doors looking for this pendant, I assume you’re going to be somewhere nearby soon. I’d prefer it if you didn’t get yourself killed in someone else’s play.”

Tearius smirked faintly. “Since when do you care whether I get killed?”

“Since you turned up in my yard as a teenager with half a bike and a very stupid plan,” Nehinhatu said. “You were scrawny, underfed, and tried to pay me in charm. It was pathetic.”

“I was not pathetic,” Tearius said. “Just… resourceful.”

“You tried to convince me the back wheel was a ‘design choice’,” Nehinhatu said. “It was on backwards.”

Tearius couldn’t help the short huff of laughter. “I remember you charged me three times what it was worth.”

“And you still came back,” Nehinhatu said. “You’re trouble, but you’re my trouble. I’d rather not have to explain to anyone why the only person mad enough to ride this thing ended up face down in a canal because he wandered into a guild operation he didn’t understand.”

Tearius tapped the note against his palm. “So. A pendant that matters. Thieves under pressure. Red Scarves getting hit. And the others think they’re just chasing pickpockets.”

“Yes,” Nehinhatu said. “And if the Guard or the wrong eyes catch them in the wrong place, they’re not going to listen to subtle distinctions like ‘we didn’t know about the pendant’.”

“Helpful as always,” Tearius said. His tone was light, but his eyes weren’t.

He looked towards the narrow gap of alley that led back to the street where Lysa’s mother lay, bleeding and confused, with a stolen locket that meant nothing to the people who took it.

“You could ignore this,” Nehinhatu said quietly. “Ride the bike somewhere else tomorrow. Find a different city with worse ale and fewer secrets. No shame in that.”

“There’s always shame in running,” Tearius said. “Question is whether it’s worth it.”

He folded the note small and slipped it into an inner pocket.

“Fine,” he sighed. “I’ll take a look. If there’s a pendant, I’d rather hear about it from you than from a man holding a knife.”

“That’s the closest thing to a sensible sentence I’ve heard from you all week,” Nehinhatu said. “Try to stay ahead of this. And Tearius?”

He hesitated.

“What?”

“If this turns ugly—and it will—remember who’s actually dangerous,” Nehinhatu said. “It’s not the street thieves. It’s the people who told them where to go.”

Tearius nodded once. “I’ll keep that in mind.”

He pushed off the wall, adjusted his hood, and headed for the alley.

He told himself it was because of the pendant.

Because information was currency.

Because walking blind into a guild pressure job was a good way to end up dead, and dead men didn’t spend coin.

It had nothing—absolutely nothing—to do with Varin’s immediate willingness to help a stranger, or the quiet certainty in Morjor’s voice, or Lysa’s shaking hands clutching that useless little locket.

Those were other people’s problems.

He slipped into the shadows and made his way back towards the street all the same.

# The Chase

Elira sat propped against the cart, red scarf darkened where Morjor had tightened fresh bandages over the worst of the bleeding. Her breathing had steadied; the colour had come back to her lips, if not her cheeks.

“You’ll be all right,” Morjor said gently. “Rest. The worst is past.”

The flour-dusted baker hovered nearby, arms folded but eyes soft. “I’ll keep an eye on her,” he said. “Girl too. You lot… you don’t look like you’re built for standing still.”

Lysa clutched her doll and looked up at Varin, eyes huge. “Please,” she whispered. “Don’t let them keep it.”

Varin crouched to her height, the movement easy despite his bulk. “We’ll do what we can,” he said. “The Light doesn’t favour cowards.”

He glanced at the baker. “Keep her here. If anyone comes asking, tell them she’s under our protection.”

Amdir raised a brow. “Is she?”

“She is now,” Varin said. “You wanted payment. This is how we earn it.”

Amdir sighed as if put upon by the universe itself. “Fine. Let’s go listen to our better natures before they get us both killed.”

The baker jerked his chin towards the east. “Thieves ran that way. Towards the alleys by the canal. One was limping—hard. You’ll know him when you see him.”

Morjor nodded his thanks. “Stay with each other,” he told Elira and Lysa. “Fear is lighter when shared.”

Then the three of them—Varin, Morjor, Amdir—turned and moved at a jog down the street.

Sheyton’s alleys tightened quickly, swallowing sound and light.

Morjor led at first, eyes on the cobbles. “Here,” he said, pointing: dark smears where boots had slipped on wet stone; a heavier impression where a weight had dragged.

“Limping,” Varin said. “The one the baker mentioned.”

They followed.

The limping thief ran like a man who knew the city but wished he didn’t have to prove it. He cut through rows of cramped houses, skidded past piles of crates, ducked under washing lines. His bad leg slowed him just enough to keep the party always in sight—but never quite close enough to grab.

They crossed a small market square, half-closed for the night. A few vendors were still packing up; one shouted as the thief shouldered past, sending a basket of bruised apples tumbling.

Varin hurdled the rolling fruit; Morjor went around them without breaking stride. Amdir stepped neatly between them, boots never slipping.

“Could’ve just stayed in the bar,” Amdir muttered as he ran. “Ale, warmth, no exercise. Glorious.”

“You could have,” Morjor replied, breathing steady. “You did not.”

“Because someone mentioned payment,” Amdir said. “Don’t make it sound like moral growth.”

The thief cut left, into a busier street where the buildings stood taller and the roofs leaned close together overhead. Lanterns hung over doorways; the glow from a nearby smithy painted one wall orange.

Up ahead, the limping man darted down a narrower side-lane, almost vanishing into shadow.

“There!” Varin called, pushing harder.

---

Tearius did not follow them directly.

He slipped through his own city instead.

While the others sprinted down main alleys, Tearius took the smaller cuts—half-forgotten servant paths, gaps between workshops, the back passages of a place you only learned if you’d had to run from things more than once.

The note in his pocket had already named the destination: an old warehouse, half-abandoned, used by the guild when they wanted to be close to the centre of town but out of the Guard’s line of sight.

He didn’t need to chase the thieves to know where they were going.

He emerged into a broader lane that ran through one of Sheyton’s older trade rows: two- and three-storey buildings pressed shoulder to shoulder, upper floors jutting slightly over the street, the sky above a narrow strip of dark.

There it was.

The warehouse sat back from the street a little, squashed between a cooper’s shop and a shuttered grain merchant. Its big double doors were scarred by years of use; one of the upper windows was boarded unevenly. A single lantern burned over the entrance, throwing a weak spill of light onto the step.

Tearius arrived just in time to see a limping figure—Jerrin—stumble down the lane and hammer on the doors with the side of his fist.

“Open up!” Jerrin hissed. “It’s me! Let me in!”

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then a bar scraped back on the other side. One door opened a crack—just enough for Jerrin to squeeze through. The door slammed. The bar dropped back into place with a solid, practised thunk.

Tearius stayed in the shadow of a recessed doorway opposite, watching.

Voices briefly rose inside—someone swore, someone else coughed—but he couldn’t make out the words.

He waited a few heartbeats more, counting silently. No one else came or went.

All right.

He stepped out of his hiding spot and crossed the street, boots quiet on the worn stone. Up close, the warehouse smelled of dust, old grain, and too many people in too small a space.

He set his hand lightly against the seam of the big doors and gave the faintest push. Solid. Whoever had barred it knew what they were doing.

“Of course,” he murmured.

He let his fingers slip down to the latch—checking for wire, for the drag of a trap line. Nothing obvious. That didn’t mean much, but it was something.

He leaned in slightly, just enough for his ear to touch old wood.

Raised voices. Arguing. Jerrin’s hoarser tone complaining. Rook’s sharper snap over the top of it all. The thieves were unsettled.

Good.

Tearius stepped back from the doors and cast a quick look up and down the street.

He wasn’t alone anymore.

At the far end of the lane, three familiar shapes came into view: Varin in front by half a stride, big shoulders squared; Morjor at his side, controlled and steady; Amdir slightly behind, coat flaring with every irritated step.

They slowed as they approached, all three taking in the building, the lantern, and the man in the hood already standing there as if he’d been waiting for them.

Varin’s expression softened just a little when he recognised Tearius. “You found them too,” he said.

“I found the place,” Tearius corrected. “They came to me. Saves on effort.”

Amdir looked from Tearius to the warehouse door, the set of the bar, the faint glow through the board gaps. “You couldn’t have opened it before we arrived?” he asked. “I was halfway prepared to be impressed.”

“It’s barred from the inside,” Tearius said. “And I prefer going into dens with at least _some_ idea of what’s waiting. Rushing in blind is more your style.”

Morjor stepped up to the edge of the lantern light, head tilting as he listened at the wood for a moment. “Anger,” he said quietly. “Fear. Seven, perhaps eight voices. They are not unified.”

“Good,” Varin said. “Divided enemies fall faster.”

Amdir exhaled, adjusting his cuffs. “Well, here we are,” he said. “One bleeding mother, one stolen locket, and an entire building full of people who don’t want to give it back.”

“Plus whatever else they’re really here for,” Tearius thought, but did not say.

He just glanced at the others, then back up at the dark shape of the warehouse.

“Door’s barred. Windows are higher than they look,” he said. “They know someone followed them. This is going to get complicated quickly.”

Varin’s tusks showed in the ghost of a grim smile. “Complicated means they’re still alive to complicate,” he said. “I can work with that.”

The four of them stood together now, just outside the pool of lantern light, the noise of the city a dull hum beyond the lane.

Inside the old warehouse, the thieves’ den seethed, unaware that their night was about to get much worse.

# The Fight
Lantern-light pooled weakly around the big double doors, leaving the corners deep in shadow. The four of them stood just beyond the circle of light—Varin, Morjor, Amdir, and Tearius—listening to the muffled arguments inside.

“Seven, maybe eight,” Morjor murmured. “Their voices pull in different directions. They are afraid.”

“Afraid people do stupid things,” Tearius said. “We should keep that in mind.”

Amdir rolled his shoulders, eyes still on the building. “Yes, yes. Fear, philosophy, all very deep. Shall we knock or just stand here until they come out and stab us anyway?”

He barely finished the sentence when something moved in the dark to their right.

A figure peeled away from the wall—one of the thieves who’d circled back, thinking to catch someone straggling. Dagger low, step light, aiming straight for Tearius’s spine.

He got three steps before Morjor’s head snapped round.

“Right,” the monk said calmly.

Tearius didn’t turn fully. He shifted his weight instead, letting instinct and training take over. As the thief lunged, Tearius stepped sideways and caught the man’s wrist, guiding the dagger past his ribs.

Morjor was there a heartbeat later, big hand closing on the thief’s shoulder. Between them, they twisted his momentum into the ground. He hit the cobbles hard, the dagger skittering away.

The thief groaned and tried to scramble up. Morjor planted a foot gently but firmly on his back.

“I’d stay down,” Morjor advised.

Tearius crouched, picking up the dropped dagger. He spun it once, considering, then flipped it to a reverse grip and rested the point just under the thief’s chin as the man turned his head.

“Let’s be civil,” Tearius said quietly. “You start by not screaming.”

The thief swallowed. “We… we didn’t know you’d follow,” he stammered. “We were just told to hit Red Scarves, all right? Check them for that bloody pendant, and if we didn’t find it—”

He cut himself off, eyes widening.

“Pendant?” Tearius repeated softly. “Interesting.”

Amdir, still by the door, exhaled loudly. “We are _outside_ their den, in the middle of a city that already hates thieves, and you’re having a long heartfelt chat. Can we please move this along before the Guard gets curious?”

Varin shot him a look, but didn’t interrupt.

Morjor eased his weight off the thief’s back. “You are outnumbered, wounded in pride, and surrounded by men who do not actually wish to kill you,” he said. “Crawl away. Choose better jobs.”

The thief blinked, clearly not expecting that.

Tearius lifted the dagger away from his throat and stood. “Go,” he said. “If anyone asks why you’re still breathing, you tell them Tearius let you crawl off instead of leaving you under a sheet.”

The thief nodded frantically. “Right. Right. I never saw you. You’re ghosts.”

He scuttled backwards, then turned and scrambled down the lane on hands and knees before finding his feet and limping away.

Amdir shook his head. “Mercy from one side, threats from the other. You two are terrible for my consistency.”

Before Tearius could answer, the barred door to the warehouse thudded.

Voices rose inside—sharper now. Then the bar lifted with a heavy scrape, and both doors flew open.

Four thieves burst out into the lane.

Rook led, lean and fast, leathers dark, twin blades already in hand. Three more followed—rag-wrapped hands, nervous eyes, weapons drawn because they were more afraid of being found without them.

Rook saw the four figures waiting for him and smiled a little too widely.

“Well,” he said. “Doorstep welcoming committee.”

He flicked his chin towards them. “Take them. Quick and loud.”

Everything moved at once.

One thief rushed Morjor. Another veered towards Amdir, seeing silk and assuming easy prey. The third hung back, crossbow half-raised, hunting a shot through the chaos.

Rook went straight for the biggest target—Varin.

---

Morjor stepped into his attacker’s path with the unhurried precision of a man sweeping aside an inconvenience.

The thief swung clumsily, panic overextending his arm. Morjor caught the man’s wrist, turned with the motion, and drove his palm into the thief’s chest. Air left lungs in a ragged wheeze. A hooked leg took the thief’s feet out from under him.

He hit the ground and stayed down.

“That is one,” Morjor said, almost regretful.

Amdir, meanwhile, found himself duelling with a thief who had far too much adrenaline and not enough talent.

The cutpurse lunged low. Amdir slipped aside, coat flaring, and the blade kissed empty air.

“You’re very enthusiastic,” Amdir said, stepping back as another strike flashed past. “Misguided, poorly paid, but enthusiastic.”

He parried with the edge of a short sword he’d drawn more from necessity than desire, their blades scraping. The thief snarled and shoved harder.

Amdir gave ground. Not because he couldn’t hold it, but because out of the corner of his eye he could see Rook and Varin, and he was already calculating how bad this was about to get.

Morjor took in the scene in a heartbeat—the thief he’d dropped, Amdir pressed harder than he liked to admit, Varin squaring up against the captain.

He moved.

He crossed the distance between himself and Amdir’s opponent in three strides, stepping in as the man drew back for another thrust. A quick strike to the elbow numbed the thief’s sword hand; a second blow to the side of the neck folded him.

The man collapsed at Amdir’s feet.

“You are welcome,” Morjor said, breathing a little harder now.

Amdir flicked imaginary dust from his shoulder. “I had that under control.”

“You were losing ground,” Morjor pointed out.

“Yes,” Amdir said, “but I was losing it elegantly.”

He didn’t get to elaborate.

---

Rook circled Varin, blades moving with easy familiarity.

“You’re in the way,” Rook said. “Get out of it and I might let you keep walking.”

Varin’s stance was solid, shield up, eyes calm. “You attacked a woman in front of her child,” he said. “There’s nowhere you can walk that takes you out of what you’ve already done.”

Rook snorted. “We were ordered to knock on a Red Scarf door,” he said. “Her lot paints targets on themselves. We were just… checking the target.”

Varin’s jaw tightened. “Your choices are still your own.”

He raised his free hand, muttering a brief prayer under his breath. A mote of fire bloomed in his palm, then streaked towards Rook.

The thief captain twisted aside. The flame scorched a black streak up the warehouse door instead.

Rook laughed. “You sure you’re aiming at me, priest?”

Varin summoned another bolt, pushing the frustration down. The Light was with him. His aim was true. It had to be.

He cast again.

The fire splashed against the cobbles this time, hissing out in a puff of steam.

“Closer,” Rook taunted. “Try hitting anything that isn’t architecture.”

Varin gritted his teeth, inhaled, exhaled. The next bolt burned in his palm, warm and eager.

“Third time,” he murmured, “for the Light.”

He loosed.

It went wide, illuminating the alley for a heartbeat as it flashed past Rook’s shoulder and vanished into the night.

Rook’s smile sharpened into something predatory. “You’re very devout,” he said. “Sadly, your god doesn’t aim for you.”

He lunged.

Varin got his shield up for the first strike; steel rang on wood and iron. The second blade flicked low, testing. Varin adjusted, still backing up.

Then Rook feinted, drew Varin’s shield a hair too far to the left, and drove his main blade into the half-orc’s side under the rim, where padding was thinner.

Pain flared bright and white. Varin’s breath hitched.

He swung in reflex, but the blow was wild. Rook stepped aside and kicked his knee.

Varin’s leg buckled. The world tilted.

He hit the ground on one knee, then fully, the cobbles hard under his shoulder. His hand spasmed; the next ember of a spell guttered out between his fingers.

“Stay down,” Rook said, breathing hard now but grinning. “You’re not worth the risk standing up.”

He turned away, already looking for his next threat.

Varin reached for his holy symbol, trying to pull breath, prayer, anything. His fingers slipped on blood.

The street tilted again, then blurred.

---

Amdir saw Varin fall and, for a heartbeat, actually weighed his options.

He could stay.

Staying meant stepping into range of a man who moved like killing was a language he’d grown up speaking. It meant risking a blade in his gut for a locket he didn’t own and a girl he didn’t know.

Or he could go.

There was an open door behind Rook now, leading into a building that probably contained both the locket and salvageable coin. There might even be a second way out that didn’t involve facing the captain head on.

Survival and profit were, for once, on the same side of the balance.

Morjor, wiping blood from his forearm where a lucky cut had opened the skin, turned to call to him.

“Amdir—”

But the elf was already moving.

“Hold the street,” Amdir said quickly. “I’ll… check inside.”

He slipped past, hugging the edge of the doorway, and vanished into the dim interior of the warehouse, leaving Morjor with an unconscious thief at his feet, a bleeding Varin on the cobbles, and a very alert Rook turning back towards them.

Morjor exhaled slowly. “Of course,” he said.

Rook wiped his blade on Varin’s robe and looked up, eyes narrowing. “One down,” he said. “Let’s see if the rest of you are as forgiving.”

Morjor shifted his stance, drawing a steadying breath, weighing his own injuries. He could take another few hits. Maybe.

He also wasn’t stupid.

He stepped back, letting the darkness of a side doorway swallow him.

If the fight was going to continue, it didn’t have to be on _this_ ground.

---

Around the side of the warehouse, Tearius had already melted into the shadows.

As Rook and the others crashed out through the front doors, Tearius had quietly reassessed his priorities.

Front door: chaos, shouting, Rook, holy men bleeding and insisting on standing their ground.

Side: quieter, darker, more likely to lead to opportunities that involved money and fewer stab wounds.

He’d chosen the side.

An old service door sat half-rotten in its frame halfway down the building. Tearius eased it open just enough to slip through.

Inside, the warehouse smelled of dust, sweat, and faint old grain. Crates were stacked haphazardly along the walls; a brazier burned low near the centre, throwing jittering shadows.

And between Tearius and that brazier were two thieves in the middle of a quiet argument.

One was a young woman with dark hair tied back in a rough knot, a short sword at her hip and blood on her hands that didn’t look like it was hers. The other was Jerrin, the limping man they’d chased here, bandage across his ribs already leaking red again.

They both froze when they saw Tearius.

Jerrin’s hand went for his knife out of habit. Mira’s went out to stop him.

“Don’t,” she hissed. “Just—don’t.”

Tearius closed the door softly behind him.

“Evening,” he said, voice low but not unkind. “Terrible night for a warehouse party.”

Jerrin’s jaw clenched. “If you’re here to collect merit badges for heroics, you’re in the wrong room.”

“I’m not here for merit,” Tearius said. “Just information. And perhaps a donation.”

Mira stepped half in front of Jerrin, eyes wide but not empty. She’d already done her crying earlier, Tearius could tell. Now there was just raw edge left.

“Please,” she said, hands half-raised. “Don’t kill him. Don’t kill me. This isn’t—we didn’t choose this.”

“So I’ve heard,” Tearius said. “Pressure from above. Something about a pendant.”

She swallowed. “We’re not robbing for coin anymore,” she said. “Coin would be simple. Coin would be… fair. This is… they want a thing. A little pendant. No one tells us what it does, only that if no one finds it, Sheyton starts burning one house at a time.”

Jerrin grimaced. “Red Scarves first,” he muttered. “Always the ones with the wrong friends who get hit when people higher up start a war.”

“So tonight you kicked in a Red Scarf family’s life looking for this pendant,” Tearius said. “And when you didn’t find it, you took whatever else you could grab so you didn’t come back empty-handed.”

Mira flinched, but didn’t deny it.

“We’re getting squeezed from both sides,” she said. “The Guard on one, the guild on the other. We don’t find the pendant, we’re dead. We do find it, we’re probably dead anyway because someone will tidy up loose ends.”

“And in the meantime, you spill other people’s blood,” Tearius said softly. “Which keeps you alive… for a while.”

Her eyes met his. There was no excuse there, only tired truth. “For a while is still preferable,” she said.

Jerrin shifted, breath catching with the pain. “Look,” he said, voice rough. “You want the locket, take it. You want the coin, take it. You want us to vanish, we can vanish. Just don’t drag a blade across our throats to make a point, yeah?”

Tearius considered them.

Two thieves. One scared, one standing scared _and_ between him and the other.

He could kill them. It would be cleaner, in some ways. Two fewer people in the mix. But it wouldn’t change the fact that someone up the chain was pushing this pendant hunt, and that ugly pressure would just fall on different necks.

He stepped closer. Both tensed—but he only held out a hand.

“Pouches,” he said. “Anything you stole that doesn’t have a little girl’s face attached to it. And this locket, if any of you still has it. You walk out of here lighter. I walk out of here richer and marginally better informed.”

Mira hesitated, then unclipped a small coin pouch from her belt. She added a silver ring and a carved set of dice that looked like they’d seen years of use. Jerrin dug into his coat and pulled out another pouch, then a battered brass locket on a broken chain.

He stared at it for a second. “This is hers,” he muttered. “The mother.”

Tearius nodded. “Good,” he said. “I know where it’s going.”

He took the locket with his left hand, slipping it into an inner pocket. The coin and trinkets went into his belt in a practised motion.

Finally, he stepped back, giving them room.

“You two are going to walk out that side door,” he said quietly. “You’re going to keep your heads down and not look back. If anyone asks you later why you’re still breathing, you tell them Tearius was in a generous mood and didn’t feel like redecorating the floor.”

Mira stared at him, stunned. “You’re just… letting us go?”

“I’m not in the habit of killing people for following orders badly,” Tearius said. “Besides, you owe me now. Debts have value.”

Jerrin let out a breath he’d been holding for what felt like years. “We won’t forget this,” he said. “I won’t.”

“Good,” Tearius replied. “Live long enough to make that useful.”

Mira sheathed her sword with shaking hands. “If you’re smart, you’ll get what you need and leave before the Guard shows,” she said. “Serel’s been sniffing around this part of town. She doesn’t care who started a mess. Only who’s standing in it when she arrives.”

“Sound advice,” Tearius said. “Try following some yourself.”

He moved past them into the deeper shadows of the warehouse, leaving them a clear path to the door he’d just used.

They didn’t hesitate. They slipped out, pulling it nearly shut behind them.

Tearius waited until the soft scuff of their footsteps faded.

Then he turned his attention to the rest of the warehouse—the stacked crates, the glint of coin on a makeshift table, the faint sound of a fight building upstairs where Rook and the others had retreated, and somewhere in there, his so-called allies getting in over their heads.

“Right,” he murmured. “Let’s see what’s worth taking before the whole board changes.”

He padded further inside, keeping low, a shadow among shadows, as upstairs the night continued to tilt towards breaking glass and the sound of a monk deciding a window was a better exit than a captain’s blade.

# The Arrest

Inside the warehouse, the noise of the street was just a dull pressure against the walls.

Tearius moved through the shadows at the back, quiet as dust. Crates were stacked in uneven rows—old trade stock, some cracked open and picked clean, others still roped shut.

He wasn’t interested in grain or mouldy cloth. He was interested in anything people had bothered to hide.

Near the rear wall he found it: a crate that didn’t quite line up with the ones beside it, a tarp thrown over carelessly, like someone had been in a hurry to pretend they hadn’t just been in this exact spot.

He pulled the tarp back.

Underneath, tucked behind a half-split crate lid, was a small stash: a velvet pouch that clinked softly when he lifted it, a handful of loose silver, and a battered brass locket on a broken chain.

He turned the locket over in his fingers. The engraving was worn—a simple spiral, almost rubbed out with years of use. When he thumbed the catch and opened it, he saw a tiny sketch inside: two adults and a girl with a familiar hair ribbon.

Lysa’s face stared back at him in miniature, smiling in better days.

“Found you,” he murmured.

The locket went into an inner pocket, separate from the coins. The pouch and silver disappeared more casually into his belt.

He let the crate fall back into place and pulled the tarp down again, more for habit than necessity. The thieves weren’t going to come checking their stash any time soon.

Footsteps traced down the central aisle of the warehouse. Measured, cautious.

Amdir’s silhouette appeared between two stacks of crates, sword still in hand, eyes adjusting to the dim.

“Of course,” he said dryly. “I risk my charming face against armed criminals, and I find you back here rearranging their savings.”

“Someone has to make sure tonight’s work pays,” Tearius replied. “You seemed busy collecting scars.”

Amdir looked him up and down. “So? Anything that looks like a cheap sentimental heirloom among the shiny things, or is our client going to be disappointed?”

Tearius’s hand brushed briefly against the pocket with the locket. “I’m working on it,” he said. “You’ll get your cut.”

Amdir narrowed his eyes but let it go. For the moment.

“Good,” he said. “Once we have the trinket, we can consider this charity work slightly less offensive.”

A shout cut across the room from above—a hoarse, angry voice, then the scrape of boots on wood and the crash of something hitting a wall.

Morjor.

---

The monk was running out of room.

He’d followed the retreating thieves up a narrow staircase, hoping to pin them or drive them down into Varin’s reach.

Instead, Rook and one remaining cutpurse had used the tight corridors against him, forcing him back step by step until his heels brushed the wall of a cramped office.

Rook’s twin blades flashed in the dim light.

“You’ve got conviction,” the captain said. “I’ll give you that.”

The last thief hovered to one side, breathing hard, knife trembling in his hand but still raised. Blood from an earlier strike soaked Morjor’s sleeve.

Conviction didn’t stop arms from getting tired.

Morjor glanced back. The office smelled of old ink and dust. A crooked desk, shelves of mildewed ledgers—and a small window, glass fogged with grime. Beyond it, a slice of Sheyton’s night and the faint orange glow of torches.

“I do not wish to kill you,” Morjor said, voice still calm. “You can lay your arms down and end this.”

Rook grinned, but his eyes were tight. “You’re in a guild safehouse,” he said. “No one walks out of here ‘ending this’. Not you, not us.”

He stepped forward.

Morjor breathed in once, slow, and made his choice.

He ducked back into the office, Rook’s blade scraping sparks off the doorframe where his shoulder had been. The captain crashed after him.

The thief hesitated at the threshold just long enough to see Morjor pivot, plant one foot on the crooked desk, and hurl himself sideways into the window.

The rotten frame never had a chance.

Glass and wood gave way with a splintering crack. The world turned into cold air and falling shards.

---

Outside, boots were already pounding.

City Guard flooded the street around the warehouse, armour clanking, shields locking into a half-ring around the front. Torches flared, throwing hard light against crooked stone.

Commander Serel Veynar stood at their centre, plate blackened and polished, greatsword at her hip. She didn’t shout. She didn’t have to.

“Surround it,” she said, voice low and absolute. “No one comes out without my leave.”

Varin lay on the cobbles where Rook had dropped him, breath shallow, blood dark on his robe. Two guards had knelt already—one pressing a cloth to the wound, the other holding a waterskin.

“You’re all right,” the younger one said, more hope than certainty in his voice. His tabard sat slightly crooked on his shoulders. “Stay with me, sir.”

Varin grimaced. “I’m… not your sir.”

“You are until you stop bleeding,” the lad replied, surprisingly firm. “After that we can argue titles.”

Serel’s gaze slid past Varin to Rook, who’d just emerged from the side alley, seeing the ring of steel and deciding very quickly that his odds inside were better than his odds running.

He dropped his blades without prompting, hands raising.

“Commander,” he said with a crooked smile. “Look, this is all a misunderstanding—”

“Bind him,” Serel said.

Rook’s smile faltered as two guards slammed him to his knees and jerked his arms behind his back.

He didn’t resist.

On the far side of the building, glass exploded outward.

Morjor plunged from the first-storey window with a burst of shattered panes and splintered frame.

One guard reacted on instinct as morjor fell out the window before him, shields coming up.

The monk crashed into the ground and two others, turning what could have been a bone-breaking impact into a chaotic tangle of armour, limbs and curses.

Morjor blinked, getting his breath back. He took in the wall of armed guards, the commander, the torches, the ring of steel.

Then he put his palms flat against the cobbles and pushed himself to his knees.

“I surrender,” he said quietly. “There has been enough fighting for one night.”

Several spears stayed trained on him. Serel watched, eyes narrowed.

“Shackle him,” she said at last. “He can talk when the envoy asks, not before.”

Iron bit into Morjor’s wrists. He didn’t resist that either.

Varin, half-propped against a crate now as the field-dressing was secured, watched through gritted teeth.

“Morjor…” he started.

The monk turned his head just enough for Varin to see the small, reassuring nod.

“We still breathe,” Morjor said. “That is more than some can say.”

Serel surveyed the scene, satisfied that the exterior was contained.

Then her attention turned to the warehouse itself.

“Anyone still inside,” she called, voice cutting through the street, “come out with your hands visible. Now.”

Silence answered her.

She exhaled once, slow.

“Very well,” she said. “We’ll drag them out instead.”

---

Inside, the warehouse felt suddenly smaller.

Tearius and Amdir both froze when Serel’s voice reached them—muted through wood, but unmistakable.

“That,” Amdir said under his breath, “is not the voice of a woman inclined to generous misunderstanding.”

Tearius listened, weighing his options.

Outside: angry commander, full ring of armed guards, a city that already suspected anyone who happened to be standing too close to trouble.

Inside: evidence of a thieves’ den, the stash he’d just looted, and more than enough circumstantial guilt to bury them if anyone with rank decided to be lazy with the details.

“We’re going to look very bad when they come in here,” he said.

“We _already_ look very bad,” Amdir replied. “We’re standing in a thieves’ warehouse surrounded by their belongings. Varin’s unconscious, Morjor’s vanished, the captain is out there telling the story his way. We are, as they say, exquisitely doomed.”

Tearius scanned the room—the crates, the brazier, the Lantern dangling from a nail on a beam, its flame guttering low.

“Unless there’s nothing here to prove it,” he said.

Amdir blinked. “Explain.”

“If the den burns,” Tearius said, thinking aloud, “there’s no stash, no ledgers, no side letters mentioning pendants, no proof we were taking anything but shelter in a burning building.”

Amdir stared at him. “Your solution to being accused of being a criminal,” he said very slowly, “is to commit arson.”

“It muddies the story,” Tearius said. “Muddy stories are harder to prosecute.”

Amdir opened his mouth to say something cutting and didn’t quite get there.

Because Tearius had already grabbed the lantern.

“Don’t—” Amdir started.

Too late.

Tearius yanked the lantern from its hook and hurled it at a stack of dry crates, half-soaked in old oil where someone had been careless with a barrel weeks before.

Glass shattered. Flame blossomed, caught, and began licking its way hungrily up the wood.

For a moment, nothing happened.

Then the fire found something worth eating.

The crate flared. Heat rolled across the room in a sudden wave. Smoke began to curl along the ceiling, thick and dark.

“Right,” Tearius said, a little too quickly. “Now we shout, the commander comes in, we act surprised, and—”

Amdir cast sleep.

His words connectedwith Tearius’s. his eyes flashed white for a heartbeat, then everything went grey. Tearius sagged to the floor, out cold, lantern-smashed glass crunching under him.

“You remarkable idiot,” Amdir hissed, shaking out his hand. “I was supposed to die of old age and good wine, not smoke inhalation and shared charges.”

The fire climbed the stack of crates, cracking and popping.

Smoke thickened.

Amdir coughed, then did the only thing he could think of that might not end with them both dead or executed.

He raised his hands and shouted.

“Fire!” he bellowed, voice cracking on the word. “Commander! There’s a fire in here and an unconscious fool who doesn’t deserve to die in it! We surrender! Get your knights in shining plates in here before the whole thing goes up!”

Outside, guards tensed. Several glanced at Serel.

Her eyes narrowed.

“Two with me,” she snapped. “Buckets. Now.”

She moved.

She strode through the front door Amdir and Rook had already broken open, cloak snapping behind her, boots ringing on the threshold. Smoke rolled out to meet her; she pushed through it without hesitation.

For a second, Amdir saw her outline only as a darker shape in the haze—then she was there, in front of him, close enough for him to feel the steady gravity of her presence.

He dropped to one knee on instinct, both hands raised, Tearius sprawled unconscious beside him.

“Put the flames out first, arrest us second,” Amdir said hoarsely. “Priorities.”

Serel stared at him for half a heartbeat, then at the burning crates.

She turned, snatched a heavy tarp off a nearby stack, and flung it over the worst of the blaze, slamming it down with brutal efficiency. A guard rushed in behind her with a sloshing bucket, dousing the edges. Another wrenched open a shutter high on the wall to vent the smoke.

Within a minute, most of the immediate fire was smothered, leaving only charred wood and angry embers.

The smoke still stung. Amdir coughed, eyes watering.

Serel turned back to him and Tearius.

Up close, her eyes were colder than the steel she wore.

“You are standing in a thieves’ den,” she said softly. “Surrounded by stolen goods. With a fire you claim you did not start.”

Amdir forced a smile that came out more like a grimace. “Would it help,” he rasped, “if I said we were here to _return_ some of it?”

Her gaze flicked down, noting the bulging belt, the displaced crates, the unconscious man who had soot on his sleeve and glass in his hair.

“Not in the slightest,” she said.

Her gauntleted hand came up, two fingers pressing lightly against Amdir’s chest.

Heat flared—dark, unnatural—and his muscles locked. He gasped as his knees hit the floor fully, body refusing to obey him.

“This is no longer about what you were doing,” Serel said. “It is about where you are and when I found you.”

She nodded to the guards. “Shackle them. These two with the others. They can explain themselves to the envoy.”

Iron closed over Amdir’s wrists. Another pair of hands dragged Tearius upright enough to loop chain around his arms.

As they were hauled to their feet, Amdir caught one last glimpse of the blackened crates and the charred patch of floor.

No den. No ledgers. No clear story.

Just smoke, ash, and four men who happened to be standing in the wrong place at someone's very deliberate time.
