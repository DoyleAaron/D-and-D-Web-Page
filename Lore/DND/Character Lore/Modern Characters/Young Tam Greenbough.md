## Appearance
A young man with unruly brown hair, earnest green eyes, and hands already showing the stains of herb work. His face is open and eager, still unmarked by the patience that characterizes his grandmother. He dresses in practical apprentice robes and always carries a gathering bag and notebook for sketching plants.

## Backstory
Tam grew up knowing he was being groomed for something. His grandmother, Root-Mother Yseult, began teaching him plant lore before he could read. By ten, he could identify fifty common herbs. By fifteen, a hundred. Now he trains formally as her apprentice, learning the deeper arts.

The pressure is immense. The Root-Mother title doesn't pass by blood—it goes to the most skilled healer in the village. But everyone expects Tam to succeed his grandmother. He's talented, certainly, but is he talented enough? Is he patient enough? He lies awake at night wondering.

## Personality
- **Eager:** Wants to learn everything immediately. Struggles with the patience the work requires.
- **Talented:** Has genuine ability with plants and remedies. The skill is real.
- **Anxious:** Carries the weight of expectations. Sometimes freezes under pressure.
- **Kind:** Genuinely wants to help people. That's what drew him to healing.

## Role in the Village
Serves as Root-Mother Yseult's primary apprentice, learning the healing arts. He assists with patients, prepares medicines, and studies the vast knowledge required for mastery. He's being evaluated constantly—not just by his grandmother, but by the entire village.

## Relationships
- **Root-Mother Yseult Greenbough:** His grandmother and mentor. He loves her but also fears disappointing her.
- **Aelindra Mistwalker:** The elven healer has offered to teach him. He's not sure he's worthy.
- **Gareth Thornroot:** The village elder who handles practical matters. Takes some pressure off Tam.
- **Other Young People:** Few remain in [Frore](../../Kingdoms/Braewood/Village/Frore.md). He's lonely sometimes.

## Notable Quote
> "Grandmother says patience is the first medicine. She's right, I know she's right. But when someone's hurting, I want to help now. Learning to wait is the hardest lesson."
